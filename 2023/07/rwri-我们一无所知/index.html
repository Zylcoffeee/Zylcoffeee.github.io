RWRI第1部分：我们一无所知

七月 10, 2023 发布在 复杂系统

本文是一系列文章（该系列由5部分组成）的第一篇，涵盖了真实世界风险研究所(RWRI)在纽约举办的为期1周的真实世界风险迷你课程。该文章涉及统计学、复杂系统理论和强观点。我在这里学到的远远超过我能充分表达的内容，所以这些笔记可能更像是我的原始笔记。

RWRI是纽约市的一门强化课程，每年举办几次，由从事或正在从事风险分析的实践者（如Nassim Taleb、Robert Frey、Raphael Douady等）主持。该团队坚信，虽然学术研究和深入研究很重要，但学术界是一个危险的真空领域，因为理论与现实相去甚远。因此，这里的一切都将具有实践性。如果你说得出，你必须能做得到。

本周的一个重要主题是“不确定性下的决策”，这意味着存在波动性高或可预测性低的时期。但好消息是：您对可能发生的事情越不确定，您采取的行动就应该越明确。

以保险业为例。当涉及到定价、储蓄、流动性等方面无法考虑的极端事件时，他们会“聘请律师”。他们确保合同针对最特殊的情况做出例外或特殊规定，以使自己免于破产。可以这么说，他们从发行版本中“剪掉尾巴”，从而最大限度地减少被破坏的风险。

在真实的风险世界中，风险的大小很重要。轻微有害的风险应该与那些可能毁掉您的风险区别对待。结果（或者对系统的影响）是唯一真正重要的事情。风险分析实际上与成本效益分析并不相同。

至关重要的是，“路径依赖”对风险很重要。如果你找了 100 名赌徒，发现第 29 号赌徒破产了，你可能会认为你得到了概率为 99/100 的积极结果。但事实并非如此，你的系统更像是一个连续 100 天下注的赌徒。如果他在第29天破产，则没有第 30 天。这是整体概率与时间概率的关系，当考虑系统的风险时，它可能会产生很大的不同。

同样，无论您如何衡量，总体“影响”并不能反映真正的风险。如果我告诉您，死于从梯子上摔下来的人数比死于埃博拉病毒的人数多 10 倍，您可能会认为对流行病的担忧明显被夸大了。然而，如果你在太空中听说地球上的所有人口都被消灭了，那么更有可能是梯子还是埃博拉病毒？风险以不同的方式表现，最坏的情况比“平均”影响更重要（稍后会详细介绍）。为了让你的系统表现良好，它必须首先生存。

系统评估 如果最终用户（系统是为那些用户构建的）不对其进行评估，系统也会腐烂。我们似乎常常认为同行评估对系统来说是健康的，但也许我们不应该这么肯定。赢得奖项（来自同行！）的餐厅似乎在之后的经营中没有很好的记录。同样，学术界的所有同行评审似乎都没有注意到可重复性危机——系统的激励并不总是与既定目标一致。 你的系统激励什么？您衡量成功的标准是什么？它实际上接近现实吗，还是只是容易测量的东西？您应该深入思考并进行实证测试，以找出哪些指标真正映射到现实。 例如，人们通常会说他们想从餐馆得到这个： • 轻柔的音乐，没有人群，友善的服务员，快捷的服务，无需排队，有空间与我的团队轻松交谈 但人们实际上聚集到哪里呢？： • 排长队、拥挤、大声、粗鲁或粗鲁的服务 正如 HCI 和设计人群长期以来所知道的那样，“你不是你的用户”。您无法在委员会会议上弄清楚系统健康状况的真正信号是什么 - 请在现实中进行测试。 强制干预 添加到系统中以改进系统（或使其更可靠！）的任何内容都会增加复杂性，从而增加不确定性和风险。我们有一个坏习惯，试图添加“更多”来解决问题，增加安全层，对系统漏洞进行创可贴等。我们不经常评估这一点，但这种增加的复杂性可以（而且确实）让事情变得更糟。只要有可能——我知道这并不总是可能——删除一些东西而不是添加它。这通常在政治上并不有利，这是减少代码库大小的高级软件工程师经常听到的，但副作用要少得多。 简而言之：更简单的系统具有更少的不确定性。他们可以做得更少让你感到惊讶。而关系到大量金钱或生命的复杂系统往往不喜欢意外。 肥尾和惊喜 在肥尾分布中，一个事件会产生不成比例的影响。单个观察结果可以显着改变均值、方差等。以下是思考它的方法： • 如果两个人的身高加起来是14英尺，那么很可能是2个7英尺高的人。11英尺高的人+3英尺高的人的组合或多或少是不可能的 • 如果两个人的财富总和为 100,000,000 美元，那么很可能一个人拥有 99,950,000 美元，另一个人拥有 50,000 美元。看到不同？ 具有相互依赖性和紧急行为的系统比前者更有可能看到后者。这意味着只需要一两件坏事就能让你陷入困境。 所有证据结构都必须在“厚尾”的情况下进行修改。 事件的背景也很重要。正如在街角突发奇想购买 100 美元的领带与作为周年纪念礼物购买 100 美元的领带不同一样，某些类型的中断或系统故障的含义或多或少取决于其具体情况。 风险策略和规避 除非你有一个可以防止破产的策略，否则你的策略就会毁掉你。“避险”不能局部化，成本效益分析是危险的。您需要进行一些设置，以便您不能如果可能的话，破产吧。 科学家在研究了风险厌恶之后得出的结论是，人类对特定风险过于偏执——他们已经将风险本地化了。那些不愿意承担小任务风险的人不会只考虑小任务，他们会将其与所有其他风险一起考虑在内。风险是复合的，就像一个骑自行车的人抽烟并骚扰黑手党——只有一个人必须杀了你。当您查看系统风险时，您是孤立地查看它们，还是与您已识别的所有其他风险一起查看？ 其他一些过去被称为“非理性”的事情在上下文中可能完全有意义。下次当您称工程师的决策“不适当”或“不合理”时，请记住这一点。举个例子，人们有时被发现有如下偏好： • 苹果 > 橙子 • 橙子 > 梨子 • 梨 > 苹果 从决策理论的角度来看，这是疯狂的，但对于考虑这两种选择的人来说却是完全合理的。为您提供的服务可以反映环境，从而鼓励您调整自己的偏好。 同样，狮子并不总是吃同样的动物——它们吃容易吃的东西。这有一个有趣的效果，因为它似乎可以防止供应过剩（只是猜测），但关键是“最好的做法”可以根据环境而变化。你的“最佳实践”在多大程度上束缚了人们，它们会以多快的速度变得陈旧？在复杂系统中正确的做法不是也永远不会是静态的。当然希望你的自动化能够处理这个问题。 指标和相关性 数字具有可比性，但商品却不可比。它们隐藏了它们的生成方式并剥夺了上下文。如果您无法重新添加上下文，您可能会遇到麻烦。 凯利准则，适应复杂的系统操作：每次发生中断时，降低风险。 您以前可能听说过虚假相关性（当纳西姆·塔勒布（Nassim Taleb）说“相关性甚至不是相关性”时，这或多或少就是他的意思），但让我给出一些对科学和工业界来说令人担忧的想法：

  1. 如果您采用两个随机生成的列表并计算它们的相关性，那么可能性会非常低。然而，创建足够多次的随机列表，您最终会得到一个显示有 99% 相关性的列表，即使这些列表是完全独立的
  2. 如果你给研究人员一台计算机，他们可以有目的地做同样的事情，一遍又一遍地运行测试，直到他们得到适合他们的故事的相关性。P-hacking 与此类似。
  3. 人们普遍认为，样本量对于相关性是否显着至关重要。然而，如果你尝试足够多的次数，无论样本大小如何，你都可以侥幸获得相关性。该大小影响的是找到一个之前的尝试次数。
  4. 数据科学基于相关性构建模型。这些机器学习初创公司中有多少家正在做严格的工作来消除虚假的相关性——如果这确实可能的话？
  5. 大数据应用程序也经常与维数灾难作斗争。任何与现实世界合作的数据科学应用程序都会遇到它。更多数据并不总是一件好事。有时你会得到“数据太多，每个变量数据不足”。
  6. 绘制人类基因组图谱并不能让我们消除所有疾病——它主要有助于解决由单一基因引起的“单基因”疾病。即使我们掌握了有关 DNA 的所有信息，变量（在本例中为相互作用的基因）仍然如此之多，以至于要预测基因变化影响输出的所有方式在计算上可能非常困难。复杂性很难。 也许我们不应该考虑不能复制为“科学”的科学。当然，这会给社会科学带来麻烦。大多数已发表的研究很可能都是错误的。再说一遍，这对同行评审过程和现代学术界的健康状况有何影响？ 有趣的是，低智商与低“成功”率相关，但高智商与“成功”完全没有相关性。不过，这是另一个话题了…… 了解随机性的最佳方法之一是进行计算机模拟。设定场景并将其播放一百或一千次。你得到了什么？您多久获得一次结果？这将有助于你的直觉。 稳定的幻觉 肥尾分布意味着更高的峰值和更肥的尾部（废话）。这实际上并不意味着罕见事件发生的频率更高，只是少量的观察结果代表了总体影响的不成比例的数量。 换句话说：波动性不是厚尾 厚尾环境中的风险可能意味着一个相当稳定的环境，其中大多数时候的故障正是您所期望的常见的、平淡的故障。除了那一次。那一次让其他所有事情变得无关紧要。系统管理员和 SRE 可能对“导致一年正常运行时间的一次中断”的形式很熟悉，尽管其严重程度可能远大于此。 注意：幂律 某些厚尾分布的一些其他特征： • 帕累托分布（听说过 80/20 规则吗？就是这样的事情）是幂律 • 幂律是尺度不变的。这意味着如果放大或缩小，事情看起来大致相同。如果20%的人拥有80%的钱，你去掉那20%，那么剩下的20%的人可能会拥有剩余的80%的钱，等等（只是一个粗略的例子） • 像这样的分布没有任何意义。您可以找到您采集的任何样本的“平均值”（样本总是有一个平均值），但不需要更多的观察即可显着改变它。稳定幂律中的均值需要非常非常长的时间。通常，它需要的数据多于可用的数据，因此它也可能是无限的。 • 一般来说，当谈论肥尾分布时，我会谈论幂律 再次强调，这些都是来自研讨会的原始笔记，几乎没有任何参考资料。我将综合这些信息并将其与我学到的其他知识相协调。结果将是……有趣的。

原文： This article is the first of a 5-part series covering the Real World Risk Institute’s 1-week mini-course on Real World Risk, held in NYC. There’s statistics, complex systems theory, and strong opinions ahead. I’ve learned far more than I can adequately represent here, so these will more or less be my raw notes.

If you want to better understand risk and decision making under uncertainty, then buckle up! Or go straight to the source and save yourself a hop :)

The content from this series is also now available in eBook form on Gumroad, Leanpub, and Amazon Kindle.

RWRI is an intensive course in NYC, held a couple times a year and run by practitioners (Nassim Taleb, Robert Frey, Raphael Douady, others)— those who have or are currently taking and analyzing risks. There is a strong belief among the group that while scholarship and deep research are important, academia is a dangerous vacuum where theories are far removed from reality. As such, everything here will have a practical bent. If you talk the talk, you must walk the walk.

Uncertainty and Distributions An important theme throughout the week was “decision making under uncertainty”, meaning times where there is high volatility or low predictability. The good news is: the more uncertain you are about what could happen, the more clear the actions you take should be.

Get used to looking at this (Source: SeekingAlpha). As a minor nitpick with the image: a fat-tailed distribution can also have a taller “middle”, but smaller “shoulders” As an example, consider the insurance industry. When it comes to extreme events that they can’t factor in to pricing, savings, liquidity, etc. they “lawyer up” — they ensure their contracts make exceptions or special previsions for the most exceptional circumstances to protect themselves from going out of business. They “clip the tails” off the distribution, so to speak, so that they minimize the risk of being ruined.

In the world of risk, the magnitude matters. Risks of minor damages or faults require an entirely different approach than those that can ruin you. Outcome (or, the affect on your system) is the only thing that really matters. Risk analysis is not really the same as cost-benefit analysis.

Critically, “path dependence” matters for risk. If you take 100 gamblers and find out that #29 goes bust, you may think you’ve got 99/100 positive outcomes — but that’s not how it works. Your system is more like a single gambler who goes in 100 days in a row. If he goes bust on day 29, there is no day 30. This is ensemble vs time probability, and when thinking about risks to your systems it can make all the difference.

Similarly, total “impact”, however you want to measure that, is not reflective of true risk. If I tell you that 10x more people die from falling off ladders than from ebola, you might be tempted to think that clearly epidemic fears are overblown. However, if you were in space and heard that the entire population of the earth was wiped out, which is it more likely to be — ladders, or ebola? Risks behave in different ways, and the worst-case scenario matters a whole lot more than the “average” impact (more on that in a bit). For your system to do well, it must first survive.

System Evaluation Systems also rot when not evaluated by their end-users (those for whom the system was built). All too often we seem to think that peer-evaluation is healthy for systems, but maybe we shouldn’t be so sure. Restaurants that win awards (from their peers!) don’t seem to have a very good track record of staying in business afterwards. Similarly, all the peer review in academia didn’t seem to notice the reproducibility crisis — the incentives of the system don’t always align with the stated objectives.

What does your system incentivize? What do you measure as success? Is it actually close to reality, or is it just something easy to measure? You should think deeply and perform empirical tests to figure out what metrics actually map to reality.

For example, people generally say they want this from restaurants:

soft music, no crowds, kind waiters, quick service, no lines, space to converse easily with my group But where to people actually flock?:

Long-lines, crowded, loud, brusque or rude service As the HCI and design crowds have known for a long time, “you are not your user”. You can’t figure out what the signals of your system health truly are in a committee meeting — test things in reality.

Compulsive Intervention Anything that is added to a system to improve it (or make it more reliable!) increases complexity, thus uncertainty and risk. We have a bad habit of trying to add “more” to fix a problem, increase the layers of safety, band-aid over a system vulnerability, etc. We don’t often evaluate this, but this added complexity can (and does) make things worse. Wherever possible — and I know it isn’t always possible — remove something isntead of adding it. This is not usually politically favorable, something that Sr. Software Engineers who reduce the codebase size have often heard, but has far fewer side effects.

Simply put: simpler systems have less uncertainty. They can do less to surprise you. And complex systems where lots of money or lives are on the line tend to not like surprises.

Fat-Tails and Surprises In a fat-tailed distribution, one event results in a disproportionate amount of the impact. A single observation can significantly move your mean, variance, etc. Here’s a way to think about it:

If the combined height of two people is 14 feet, it is most likely 2 7 foot tall people. A 11 foot tall person + 3 foot tall person combination is more or less impossible If the combined wealth of two people is $100,000,000, it is most likely one person with $99,950,000 and another with $50,000. See the difference? Systems with interdependencies and with emergent behavior are far more likely to see the latter than the former. That means it could take just one or two bad events to sink you.

All the structure of evidence must be revised under fat-tails.

Context of events also matters. Just as buying a $100 tie on a whim on a street corner is not the same as a $100 tie as an anniversary present, certain types of outages or system failures mean more or less depending on their context.

Risk Strategies and Aversion Unless you have a strategy that prevents ruin, your strategy will ruin you. “Risk aversion” cannot be localized, and cost-benefit analysis is dangerous. You need to set things up so that you can’t go bust, if at all possible.

Scientists have, after studying risk aversion, concluded that humans are overly paranoid about specific risks — they have localized the risk. No one who is risk averse with a small task is thinking about only the small task, they’re factoring it in with all of their other risks. Risk compounds, like a biker who smokes and harasses the Mafia — only one has to kill you. When you look at your system’s risks, do you look at them in isolation, or in context with all the other risks you’ve identified?

Some other things that have been called “irrational” in the past can make perfect sense in context. Keep that in mind the next time you call an engineer’s decisions “inappropriate” or “irrational”. As an example, humans have been found to sometimes have preferences like the following:

Apple > Orange Orange > Pear Pear > Apple This is madness from a Decision Theory perspective, but can make perfect sense to the person considering the two options. What you are offered can be reflective of the environment, and thus encourage you to adapt your preference.

Similarly, Lions don’t always eat the same animals — they eat what’s easy. This has an interesting effect in that it seems to prevent oversupply (just speculation), but the point is that the “best thing to do” can shift according to the environment. How much do your “best practices” handcuff people, and how quickly do they grow stale? The right thing to do in a complex system is not and never will be static. Sure hope your automation can handle that.

Metrics and Correlation Numbers are comparable, but goods are not. They hide how they were generated and are stripped of context. If you can’t add the context back in, you might be in trouble.

The Kelley Criterion, as adapted to complex system operations: every time you have an outage, reduce your risk.

You’ve probably heard of spurious correlations before (when Nassim Taleb says “correlation isn’t even correlation”, this is more or less what he means), but let me give some thoughts that are worrisome for science and industry:

If you take two randomly generated lists and calculate their correlation, odds are it will be pretty low. Create random lists enough times, however, and you’ll eventually get one that says there’s a 99% correlation, even though the lists are completely independent If you give a researcher a computer, they can do the same on purpose, running tests over and over until they get a correlation that suits their story. P-hacking is similar to this. It is commonly understood that sample size is what matters for correlation to be significant. However, if you try enough times you can get a correlation by fluke no matter the sample size. What that size affects is the number of attempts before you find one. Data Science builds models based on correlation. How many of these ML startups are doing rigorous work that weeds out spurious correlation — if indeed it’s even possible? Big Data applications also struggle often with the curse of dimensionality. Any Data Science application working with the real world will encounter it. More data isn’t always a good thing. Sometimes you get “too much data, not enough data per variable”. Mapping the human genome did not allow us to eliminate all diseases — it has mostly helped with “monogenic” ones that are caused by a single gene. Even with all the info we have about DNA, there are so many variable (interacting genes in this case) that it can be computationally intractable to predict all the ways a gene change will affect the output. Complexity is hard. Perhaps we should not consider science that doesn’t replicate to be science. That would spell trouble for the social sciences, of course. It’s quite possible that most published research is false. Again, what does that say about the peer review process and the health of modern academia?

Interestingly, low IQ correlates to low “success” rates, but high IQ has no correlation to “success” at all. That’s a topic for another time though…

One of the best ways to learn about randomness is to play around with computer simulations. Set up scenarios and play them out a hundred or a thousand times. What do you get? How often do you get that result? It will help your intuition.

The Illusion of Stability Fat-tailed distributions imply higher peaks and fatter tails (duh). That doesn’t actually mean that the rare events happen more frequently, only that a small number of observations represents a disproportionate amount of the overall effect.

In other words: volatility is not fat-tails

Risk in a fat-tailed environment may mean a quite stable environment, where most of the time the failures are exactly the common, pedestrian ones you expect. Except that one time. That one time that renders all the others inconsequential. Sysadmins and SREs may be familiar with this in the form of “that one outage that killed the uptime for the year”, although the magnitude can be much greater than that.

Note: Power Laws Some other characteristics of certain fat-tailed distributions:

A pareto distribution (ever heard of the 80/20 rule? This is that sort of thing) is a power law Power laws are scale invariant. That means if you zoom in or zoom out, things look roughly the same. If 20% of the people have 80% of the money, and you remove those 20%, then 20% of the remaining people will probably have 80% of the remaining money, etc. (just a rough example) Distributions like this have no mean. You can find a “mean” for any sample you take (samples always have a mean), but it won’t take many more observations to change it significantly. It takes much, much longer to stabilize a mean in a power law. Often it takes more data than is available, so it may as well be infinite. Generally speaking, when talking about a fat-tailed distribution, I’ll be talking about a power law Once again, these are raw notes from the workshop with almost no references.
